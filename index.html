The Macro View

The Economic Path

Q1: How strongly does annual U.S. GDP growth correlate with annual S&P 500 returns?

This connected scatter plot illustrates the complex relationship between the real economy and the stock market. While there is a general positive association, strong economic output often supports corporate earnings, the correlation is not perfectly linear. The chaotic nature of the path suggests that while GDP growth provides a fundamental floor, annual S&P 500 returns are heavily influenced by valuation multiples and investor sentiment, meaning the market can sometimes disconnect from the immediate economic reality.

Market Performance: Recession vs Expansion

Q2: During years of negative GDP growth (recessions), how does the S&P 500 typically perform compared to expansion years?

By categorizing the data into economic phases, we see a stark contrast in performance distributions. During expansion years (green), the S&P 500 consistently posts positive median returns. In contrast, recession years (red), defined by negative GDP growth, typically coincide with negative market returns or significantly higher downside risk. This confirms that economic contraction is the primary driver of major market drawdowns.

Inflation & Interest Rates

Inflation vs Returns (Colored by Interest Rate)

Q3: Is there a negative correlation between U.S. inflation rate and S&P 500 yearly returns?

This scatter plot investigates the risk of purchasing power erosion. Historically, there is a noticeable negative correlation between high inflation and S&P 500 real returns. As inflation rises (moving right on the X-axis), annual returns tend to compress. The color gradient further reveals that high-inflation periods often trigger higher Federal Funds Rates (yellow/green points), which discount future cash flows and put downward pressure on stock prices.

Fed Funds Rate vs Market Volatility

Q6: Is there a measurable correlation between the Federal Funds Rate and S&P 500 volatility?

This chart explores the link between the cost of money and market anxiety. While not a straight line, the trend suggests a measurable correlation where higher Federal Funds Rates often align with elevated market volatility. Markets tend to be calmest when monetary policy is accommodative and predictable; conversely, as the Fed tightens rates to combat overheating, the resulting liquidity drain often leads to jerkier, more volatile price action.

S&P 500 Returns: High vs Low Interest Rate Environments

Q5: How does the S&P 500 respond to rising interest rates?

Comparing high versus low interest rate environments, this boxplot demonstrates that the S&P 500 has historically performed more robustly when rates are lower. "Low Rate" environments facilitate cheaper corporate borrowing and higher asset valuations, leading to higher median returns. Conversely, when the Fed Funds Rate is above the median ("High Interest Rates"), the cost of capital increases, which tends to constrict liquidity and lower the ceiling for annual equity returns.

Market Breadth & Correlations

Economic Indicator Correlation Matrix

How do the key economic variables relate to each other?

The "Economic Indicator Correlation Matrix" quantifies the complex interplay between macroeconomic variables and market performance using a heatmap visualization where red indicates positive correlation and blue indicates negative. The data highlights a robust positive relationship between GDP Growth and Inflation (0.81), suggesting that economic expansion often comes with rising prices. Crucially for investors, the matrix reveals a significant negative correlation between Inflation and S&P 500 Returns (-0.56), indicating that higher inflation environments historically present headwinds for equity performance. Additionally, the positive correlation between Unemployment Rate and S&P 500 Volatility (0.69) underscores how labor market instability often translates into increased market turbulence.

Predictive Power: Last Year's GDP vs This Year's Market

Q9: Can lagged economic indicators (e.g., last-year GDP, inflation) improve prediction of next-year S&P 500 returns?

This plot tests the "rear-view mirror" approach to investing by comparing last year's GDP growth against this year's market return. The scatter is widely dispersed with a weak trendline, suggesting that lagged economic indicators have limited predictive power for future returns. Because the stock market is a forward-looking mechanism, it effectively "prices in" economic data as it happens, rendering last year's GDP figures largely irrelevant for predicting next year's stock movements.

Change in Unemployment vs S&P 500 Returns

Q7: Is there a correlation between unemployment rate changes and S&P 500 annual returns?

This diverging scatter plot highlights the inverse relationship between labor market health and stock performance. Generally, positive values on the X-axis (unemployment rising) correlate with negative S&P 500 returns (red dots). This confirms that deteriorating labor conditions signal weak consumer demand, which translates to lower corporate earnings and negative stock market performance.

Market Breadth: Individual Stocks vs Index

How does the distribution of individual stock returns compare to the S&P 500 index?

This analysis compares the distribution of annual returns for individual stocks (represented by boxplots) against the aggregate performance of the S&P 500 index (magenta line) for the years 2017 and 2018. The visualization highlights the significant dispersion in single-stock performance, particularly in 2017 where returns ranged from significant losses to triple-digit gains. In contrast, the S&P 500 line consistently tracks the central tendency of the market, effectively illustrating how index investing mitigates idiosyncratic risk by smoothing out the extreme volatility associated with individual stock selection.

S&P 500 Monthly Price Action

What has been the historical price trajectory of the S&P 500?

The "S&P 500 Monthly Price Action" chart visualizes the index's long-term trajectory from 2016 through late 2025, utilizing a candlestick format to capture monthly volatility. The data reveals a dominant bullish trend, with the index rising from approximately 2,000 to nearly 7,000 over the decade, though this growth is punctuated by significant market events such as the sharp, rapid decline in early 2020 and the sustained correction throughout 2022. Despite these periods of bearish pressure (indicated by red candles), the robust sequence of green candles in the subsequent recovery phases underscores the market's historical resilience and momentum, leading to new all-time highs by the end of the observed period.

S&P 500 Proxy Analysis

How well do different indicators track or predict S&P 500 movements?

This "S&P 500 Proxy Analysis" evaluates the relationship between the broader market index and five of its most influential constituentsApple, Amazon, Google, JPMorgan, and Microsoftbetween January 2016 and January 2018. The top panel illustrates normalized price trends (Base 100), revealing that while stocks like Amazon and Microsoft significantly outperformed the index by the end of the period, the group as a whole moved in lockstep with the market's general trajectory. This strong alignment is quantified in the bottom panel, where all five stocks display exceptionally high correlation coefficients ranging from 0.95 to 0.97, confirming that these major equities acted as reliable proxies for the S&P 500's overall direction during this timeframe.
